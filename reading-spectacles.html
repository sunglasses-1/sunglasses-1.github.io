Understanding Your Prescription and Pupillary Distance (PD)

Breaking down the numbers: reading an eyeglass prescription.

How to measure pupillary distance at home.

Once you have that piece of paper in your hand, it might look like a secret code. But it's pretty straightforward once you know what you're looking at. Understanding your prescription and Pupillary Distance (PD) is essential for a successful purchase.

Let's break down the key terms on your prescription:

  • SPH (Sphere): This number indicates the lens power needed to correct nearsightedness (-) or farsightedness (+).
  • CYL (Cylinder) & Axis: These values correct for astigmatism, which is when your eye is shaped more like a football than a baseball. The CYL is the power needed, and the Axis is the orientation of that correction.
  • Add: You'll only see this if you need bifocals or progressive lenses. It's the added magnifying power for the bottom part of the lens to help with reading.

Your Pupillary Distance (PD) is the distance, in millimeters, between the centers of your pupils. This measurement is crucial because it tells the lab exactly where to center the optical sweet spot of your lenses. If the PD is wrong, your vision will be off, and you could experience eye strain.

While some online retailers like Zenni provide guides on how to measure your PD at home, the most foolproof method is to get it from the professionals. When you get your eye exam, specifically ask for your PD measurement. If you forget, just call your eye doctor's office afterward; they are required to provide it to you.

Selecting the Right Lenses

Single-Vision vs. Progressive Lenses

Understanding Lens Materials and Coatings

The frames get the attention, but the lenses do all the work. Understanding your options here is crucial for both your vision and your wallet.

Your prescription will determine this, but it's good to know the terminology. Single-vision lenses correct for one field of vision (either near or distance) and are the standard, cheapest option included with most base prices.

Progressive lenses are essentially no-line bifocals, offering a smooth transition between distance, intermediate, and reading prescriptions in one lens. While they cost more, ordering progressive lenses online is significantly cheaper than buying them from a traditional optometrist's office.

This is where the add-on costs can pile up. The base price for your glasses usually includes basic plastic lenses.

  • Polycarbonate Lenses: This is a popular upgrade. They're more impact-resistant and lighter than basic plastic. For context, this is the material included in BJ's $60 basic lens package.
  • What is a high-index lens?: If you have a very strong prescription, you'll want to consider high-index lenses. They are designed to be much thinner and lighter, preventing that thick "coke bottle" look. This is almost always an extra charge.

Here's my personal advice: "Remember, each of these add-ons cost money... I keep my costs low because I don’t add any extras to my order other than thinning." I always opt for high-index lenses because my prescription is strong, but I skip coatings like anti-glare or blue-light filtering to manage the final price.

Paying for Your New Glasses

Using FSA for Online Glasses

Getting Reimbursed Through Vision Insurance

The final step is payment. Thankfully, online retailers have made it incredibly simple to use your benefits, whether it's an FSA card or vision insurance.

If you have a Flexible Spending Account (FSA) or Health Savings Account (HSA), you're in luck. Prescription eyewear is a qualified medical expense.

Almost every major online glasses retailer, including warehouse clubs like BJ's, makes it easy to use these funds. Usually, you can use your FSA/HSA card just like a regular credit card at checkout. For specific steps, I always recommend double-checking the retailer's FAQ page.

Using vision insurance online can be a little different. Most online sellers are considered "out-of-network." This means you'll typically pay for the glasses upfront with your own money.

After your purchase, you submit an itemized receipt to your insurance provider for reimbursement. Retailers like BJ's Optical work with major plans like VSP, EyeMed, and DavisVision for reimbursement claims. The key is to request a detailed receipt and fill out your insurance company's claim form. It’s an extra step, but the savings from ordering online are often worth it.
